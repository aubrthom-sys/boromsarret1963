Borom Sarret

Film Analysis
Analytical Analysis

00:16

Although the woman's labor occupies the background of the frame, it is essential to Sembène's representation of work in Borom Sarret. As she pounds grain in a large bowl, she performs the everyday labor necessary to sustain the household, yet this work remains unwaged and largely invisible. By framing her labor alongside the man's prayer without privileging one activity over the other, Sembène presents domestic work as an ordinary, continuous part of everyday life rather than an exceptional event. The scene encourages viewers to recognize that labor extends beyond wage-earning occupations to include the routine work that makes daily survival possible. This sequence can be understood through the lens of Marxist-feminist social reproduction theory, which argues that the household is itself a productive site. As Kirstin Munro explains, "the unwaged productive efforts of household members may include activities such as... preparing food, transmission of knowledge and skills, cleaning one's self and one's environment, and maintenance or making repairs" (Munro 2019, 463). The woman's labor exemplifies this unwaged productive effort. Although she receives no wage, her work transforms raw materials into food that sustains the household and reproduces the labor-power necessary for participation in the broader economy. Rather than depicting domestic labor as separate from economic production, Sembène reveals it to be one of its essential foundations. Reading this moment through social reproduction theory expands the film's critique of labor beyond the cart driver's struggle for paid employment. Sembène demonstrates that exploitation is not confined to the marketplace but also operates within the household, where reproductive labor is expected yet rarely acknowledged as work. By introducing domestic labor before the narrative follows the cart driver's search for income, the film establishes labor as a broad social relation encompassing both visible wage labor and the hidden reproductive work that sustains workers and their families. This brief shot therefore foreshadows one of Borom Sarret's central concerns: the unequal valuation of different forms of labor in post-independence Senegal. 00:23

Although no one is identified individually, the shot foregrounds the collective rhythms of urban labor. The pedestrians and cyclists are not presented as anonymous background figures but as participants in the daily circulation of the city. Their movement suggests that labor begins long before work itself; traveling through the city is part of the process of earning a living. By lingering on these ordinary routines, Sembène emphasizes that economic life is constituted through the repetitive, everyday activities of working people rather than through exceptional moments or elite institutions. The absence of a privileged protagonist in these opening shots is also significant. Rather than centering an individual hero, Sembène first introduces the collective social world in which labor takes place. The city's streets become spaces of movement, exchange, and survival, foreshadowing the cart driver's own dependence on navigating Dakar in search of passengers. This attention to the everyday aligns with Sembène's broader commitment to portraying the lived experiences of Senegal's working class and the structural conditions that shape their lives.

00:29

The coexistence of multiple modes of transportation is significant. Automobiles, bicycles, and pedestrians occupy the same urban space, reflecting the uneven distribution of resources and mobility within the city. While automobiles symbolize greater economic privilege and access, bicycles and walking represent more affordable means of transportation available to much of the working population. This visual contrast foreshadows the precarious position of the cart driver, whose horse-drawn cart occupies an even more marginalized place within Dakar's transportation economy. By embedding the credits within this dynamic urban environment, Sembène establishes labor, movement, and class inequality as the social context through which the rest of the film should be understood.

00:59

The cart driver's prayer immediately frames his work as precarious. Rather than asking for prosperity or success, he asks Allah for protection "from the law and infidels," revealing that legal authority is experienced not as a source of security but as a potential threat to his livelihood. Before he has even begun his day's work, the audience learns that survival depends not only on labor itself but also on avoiding the institutions capable of disrupting it. This brief prayer foreshadows the film's later conflict with the police and suggests that the state's relationship to working people is fundamentally coercive rather than protective.

01:07

The cart driver’s movement from prayer to preparation frames labor not simply as an act performed in the marketplace, but as something that must be continually reproduced before work even begins. After asking Allah to “protect me and mine from the law and infidels,” he carefully gathers his belongings (bracelet, crossbody bag, and hat) before exiting the home. The string music that enters the soundtrack underscores this transition, not as dramatic elevation, but as a tonal marker of entry into another cycle of precarious work. Sembène slows the moment of departure, emphasizing that labor is not a single event but a repetitive daily process of preparation, survival, and endurance. From a Marxist perspective, this scene foregrounds the way labor-power must be reproduced before it can be sold. As Marx writes, “The value of labour-power is determined… by the labour-time necessary for the production, and consequently also the reproduction, of this specific article” (Marx 1867, 6). The cart driver cannot simply begin working; his capacity to labor is continuously produced through the domestic sphere: food, rest, care, and the unwaged labor performed by the woman in the household. Her ongoing domestic work is what allows his labor-power to exist each day as something that can be sold in the city. The scene therefore links domestic space and wage labor as part of a single circuit of reproduction. While the cart driver prepares to enter the streets, the woman remains engaged in sustaining the household that makes his labor possible. Sembène’s composition refuses to isolate wage labor as the only meaningful form of work; instead, it shows that the “moment” of labor in the marketplace is dependent on an entire unseen infrastructure of daily reproduction. The film thus begins to establish labor not as an individual act, but as a continuous social process that moves between home, street, and economy.

01:32

This shot functions as a visual hinge between the domestic sphere and the sphere of wage labor. The cart driver’s movement through the backyard gate marks not just a physical relocation but a shift in the social organization of labor: from reproduction inside the household to circulation in the urban economy. Sembène lingers on this movement, refusing to cut directly to work, thereby emphasizing that labor is produced through transitions and spatial thresholds rather than isolated moments of production. In Marxist terms, this movement helps clarify how labor-power becomes active labor only after it is socially and materially set into motion. The household produces and sustains the cart driver’s capacity to work, but that capacity must still be carried, literally moved, into the sphere where it can be exchanged for wages. The act of leading the horse toward the cart becomes the final stage in the reproduction of labor-power before it enters circulation. The gate itself is significant as a spatial marker. It does not simply separate inside and outside; it regulates the passage between unwaged reproductive labor and waged productive labor. The cart, waiting beyond the household threshold, functions as the material embodiment of this transition: it is both the means of labor and the vehicle through which labor-power enters the city. Sembène thus visualizes what is often abstract in Marxist theory: the movement from reproduction to production is not automatic or invisible, but enacted through everyday physical gestures. By slowing down this passage, the film reinforces its broader claim that labor is not confined to the moment of earning wages. It is a continuous process that begins in the household, passes through thresholds like this gate, and extends into the city’s economic circulation.

02:28

This scene introduces a subjective layer to the cart driver’s labor through internal monologue, revealing how economic relations are constantly negotiated within personal and social obligations. His question, “When’s she going to pay me?”, initially frames labor in transactional terms, but this is immediately complicated by his acknowledgment that “she has her troubles too.” This tension highlights the instability of exchange relations in conditions of precarity, where payment is expected but not guaranteed, and where survival often depends on informal networks rather than fixed contracts. The cart driver’s decision to help the woman onto the cart further demonstrates how labor in this economy is not strictly governed by immediate remuneration. Instead, economic exchange is embedded within social recognition and mutual vulnerability. The cart becomes a shared space of circulation rather than a purely commercial vehicle, temporarily reorganizing labor into a collective arrangement shaped by circumstance rather than formal wage relations.

02:55

This sequence intensifies Sembène’s exploration of labor as a process defined by constant movement, interruption, and negotiation. The alternating camera positions (front-facing, rear-tracking, and insert close-ups) fragment the cart’s movement into a series of labor-signifying components. The cart is no longer a stable object but a system in motion: wheels, hooves, passengers, and faces all become discrete visual units of labor in circulation. The cart driver’s internal monologue reveals the contradictory social logic of his work. His description of Mamadou as “that pest” is immediately undermined by the reality that the boy is part of the same precarious labor economy. The refusal, “I won’t stop!”, is less an assertion of authority than an expression of economic urgency. Stopping means losing time; losing time means losing income. Labor here is structured not by efficiency but by continuous exposure to interruption. This is further reinforced by the driver’s comment that Mamadou “ought to stay home” despite being unemployed. The irony is central: there is no stable “home” outside labor. The boy’s daily attempt to board the cart reflects the same structural condition the driver himself inhabits: an economy in which survival requires persistent movement even when no work is guaranteed. Labor-power in this world is not activated once but repeatedly tested through daily exposure to uncertainty.

04:36

This scene stages the disabled man’s exclusion from the cart economy as a confrontation with what counts as “productive life.” His crawling entry into the frame and his repeated plea, “have pity on me”, is met with refusal and dismissal, marking him as outside the circuit of circulation that organizes the cart driver’s world. The driver’s response reduces him to a figure of excess: “There are so many beggars! They’re like flies!” Halberstam’s formulation helps clarify what is at stake in this moment. As she writes, “we might think about failure as a way of refusing to acquiesce to dominant logics of power and discipline and as a form of critique” (Halberstam 2011, 88). In Sembène’s scene, however, failure is unevenly distributed. The disabled man is not able to transform his exclusion into critique; instead, he is rendered as surplus life, excluded from both wage labor and informal circulation. His body exposes the limits of an economic system that defines value through mobility, productivity, and exchangeability. The cinematography reinforces this hierarchy through alternating high and low angles, placing the cart above and the crawling body below. Labor here is not simply exploitation but a boundary-making process that determines which bodies are legible as economic actors and which are cast out of circulation altogether.

05:57

This scene marks a structural pivot in the film from the circulation of labor and commodities to the urgent demands of social reproduction, specifically pregnancy, care, and medical survival. The interruption of the cart’s movement (“Wagoner, stop!”) breaks the earlier logic of continuous circulation and replaces it with a different temporality: one organized around bodily vulnerability rather than economic flow. The pregnant woman’s body becomes the center of the scene, not as a productive laboring body in the wage sense, but as a site where reproduction of life itself is visibly at stake. The husband’s request, “Take my wife to the maternity clinic!”, reconfigures the cart from a transport service into an emergency reproductive infrastructure. Labor is temporarily reorganized around care rather than exchange.

06:29

The pregnant woman’s gesture, resting her head on the driver’s shoulder, introduces an intimate form of care that is immediately misread by the driver as irritation and moral commentary. His monologue (“Modern women! I’ll never understand them!”) displaces the actual conditions of exhaustion and pregnancy into a gendered critique, revealing how patriarchal interpretation overlays and distorts the material reality of reproductive labor. At the same time, the driver is not simply outside care work; he is actively participating in it. Alongside the husband, he physically helps move the woman from cart to hospital steps. This complicates any strict division between productive and reproductive labor. The cart driver is simultaneously a worker in circulation and a participant in social reproduction, temporarily absorbed into a care infrastructure that exceeds wage logic. In this scene, the maternity hospital becomes the institutional endpoint of that reproductive chain, where bodily reproduction is temporarily stabilized through medical intervention. The cart’s labor is thus not separate from reproduction: it is one of the informal infrastructures that makes formal reproductive institutions accessible. The final sequence of helping the woman into the hospital, payment exchange, camera movement up the stairs, and the cut to weeds on the ground creates a vertical descent from institutional structure back to material remainder. The hospital represents formalized care and state infrastructure; the weeds/flowers return us to neglected, marginal life at ground level. This contrast quietly undercuts the idea that reproduction is fully resolved by institutional arrival.

07:57

This sequence organizes time around a central contradiction: rest is never fully separate from labor, and food is never fully separate from work. The cart driver’s lunch is not a structured break but an improvised act of consumption: kola nuts given by his wife, eaten while leaning against the cart. What appears as rest is still embedded in circulation; the body pauses, but the system does not. The driver’s statement, “I won’t get home for lunch”, marks a key displacement of the household as a stable site of reproduction. Food, care, and rest are no longer anchored in the home but redistributed across fragmented urban space. Lunch becomes something carried, exchanged, and consumed in transit rather than within a domestic setting. The cross-directional movement of cars and pedestrians reinforces this fragmentation. There is no single unified direction of urban life; instead, multiple temporalities and velocities coexist within the same frame. The cart driver’s monologue, “What’ll they eat at home? Who knows?”, extends this uncertainty back into the household, underscoring the instability of subsistence itself. If his lunch is reduced to kola nuts, then the household meal is equally uncertain, deferred, or unknown. Ultimately, the scene shows that even rest is circulatory: the cart driver’s pause is temporary, spatially exposed, and materially insufficient.

08:28

In this sequence, ancestral identity is invoked precisely at the moment of economic exchange. The cart driver’s monologue constructs a narrative of nobility, “the brave warriors of the past”, that attempts to locate dignity outside present conditions of labor exploitation. However, this invocation of ancestral lineage is immediately embedded in a scene structured by payments, performances, and transactional relations. Villagers gather, smile, and watch, yet this collective attention does not dissolve the underlying economic structure. Instead, it produces a moment where cultural expression becomes directly monetized through the driver’s repeated payments. The exchange of money punctures the driver’s monologue of inherited nobility, revealing that even narratives of dignity are embedded within economic relations. The shoe shiner’s presence intensifies this contrast. His silent labor of bending, polishing, and waiting occurs without recognition or narrative framing. When the client leaves without acknowledgement and even kicks the platform, the scene exposes a stark asymmetry: some forms of labor are rewarded through performance and exchange, while others are structurally invisible, treated as disposable service. This juxtaposition underscores the uneven valuation of labor within the same street economy.

09:47

This sequence reveals that economic exchange is not sufficient to guarantee access to basic social rites like burial. Even though the father can pay the driver, that payment does not translate into access to the cemetery. Instead, a second regime of circulation appears: one governed by documentation, belonging, and administrative legitimacy. The father’s line, “I’m a stranger here”, is crucial. It marks him as excluded not from economic exchange but from institutional recognition itself. He can participate in market relations (he can pay for transport), but he cannot enter the formal infrastructure of death rites.  The refusal at the cemetery gate (“wagoner, stay out!”) is especially significant because it marks the boundary between circulation and exclusion. The cart driver, who normally mediates movement across the city, is himself barred from the final destination of movement: burial. His labor is suddenly revealed as partial: he can transport bodies, but he cannot guarantee their social integration or ritual completion. The father’s final question, “What’s to become of my child?”. remains unanswered, and the film refuses to resolve it. Instead, the narrative shifts back to the cart driver, who abandons the scene entirely. His final line, “I have to pee”, is striking precisely because it collapses the solemnity of death into bodily necessity. It undercuts any sense of narrative closure and returns the viewer to the most basic level of embodied life.

11:16

This sequence reveals that labor is governed not only by economic necessity but by the colonial organization of urban space. The wagoner immediately recognizes “The Heights” as forbidden territory, responding not with curiosity but with alarm: “Wagoners aren't allowed up there.” His work depends on mobility, yet that mobility is sharply limited by spatial regulations inherited from colonial rule. The city is divided into zones of permitted and prohibited movement, making labor contingent upon access to space. The suited passenger occupies a different position within this hierarchy. His claim that he has "connections" suggests that access to The Heights is mediated by privilege and social capital rather than equal citizenship. Money becomes a means of persuading the wagoner to assume the risk, but it does not erase the prohibition itself. The wagoner's internal prayer, "May all the saints protect me! I'll chance it!", underscores that crossing into the district is experienced as a dangerous transgression rather than an ordinary job. Frantz Fanon's analysis of colonial space provides a powerful framework for understanding this moment. Fanon writes, “The colonial world is a world divided into compartments” (Fanon 1963, 38). Sembène visualizes this compartmentalization through the abrupt cut from the crowded streets of the African quarter to the open beaches and orderly architecture of The Heights. The spatial transition is not merely aesthetic; it exposes how colonial power survives through the unequal organization of the city, where movement, work, and belonging remain unevenly distributed after political independence.

12:40

The Heights is defined by wide streets, planned buildings, and orderly movement, while the wagoner's earlier environment was characterized by informal labor, dense neighborhoods, and constant bodily effort. Although political independence has been achieved, the spatial organization of colonial privilege persists. The close-up of the wagoner's face is especially significant because it humanizes his encounter with this unequal landscape. His curiosity is repeatedly interrupted by the squeaking wheel, a reminder that he cannot simply become part of this world. The cart's deteriorating infrastructure intrudes upon his admiration, symbolizing the material realities of his class position. His renewed prayer, "Saints, protect me!", shows that his awareness of danger has not disappeared despite the district's outward beauty.

13:26

This sequence exposes how colonial power persists not only through spatial segregation but through bureaucratic enforcement and everyday policing. The whistle violently interrupts the wagoner's movement, transforming an ordinary transport job into a disciplinary encounter. His labor is criminalized not because of what he has done, but because of where he has entered. The officer's declaration, "No carts are allowed here!", makes explicit that mobility itself is regulated according to colonial hierarchies. The most powerful image in the sequence is the officer placing his foot over the fallen medal. Throughout the film, the wagoner's possessions are few, making the medal one of his only visible personal objects. Whether understood as a military decoration, family keepsake, or symbol of honor, the medal represents dignity and personal history. By physically covering it with his boot, the officer does more than prevent the wagoner from retrieving it: he visually subordinates the wagoner's identity to the authority of the colonial state. The wagoner's clenched fist suspended above the officer's shoe captures the tension between resistance and enforced submission.

14:23

This sequence reveals the full consequences of the wagoner's encounter with colonial authority. Having lost his cart to pay the fine, he has been separated from the means through which he earned his living. Although he still possesses the capacity to work, he no longer possesses the material instrument necessary to transform labor into income. The cart's absence renders his labor-power economically useless.  His monologue initially attempts to assign blame: to the passenger who fled without paying, to the grieving father, to the storyteller, but ultimately arrives at a broader realization that "we all work for nothing." This marks an important shift from individual grievance to structural critique. The wagoner recognizes that his suffering is not caused by isolated acts of dishonesty but by an economic and political system that repeatedly extracts labor without guaranteeing security. Karl Marx captures this contradiction when he writes: "The worker becomes all the poorer the more wealth he produces" (Marx 1844, 18). Throughout the film, the wagoner works continuously, transporting passengers, hauling construction materials, carrying the sick, and assisting the grieving, yet each act of labor leaves him no more secure than before. His final dispossession demonstrates that relentless work alone cannot overcome the structural inequalities that govern colonial and capitalist society.

The change in music marks the wagoner's return to a familiar social world. The music works alongside the images of neighborhood life to distinguish the African quarter not simply as a different location, but as a different social environment grounded in shared experience and community.

17:09

The film ends where it began: within the household. This circular structure emphasizes that the failures of the day's labor are ultimately borne within the home. Although the wagoner has worked continuously, he returns unable to provide food or income, admitting, "I don't have anything for you. Not even a cart!" His inability to reproduce the conditions of his family's survival immediately shifts responsibility onto his wife, who leaves in search of food while he remains behind caring for the crying infant. This scene recalls the opening images of the wife performing domestic work. Throughout the film, her labor remains largely unwaged and invisible, yet it is essential to the family's survival. Sembène visualizes this insight by repeatedly showing the wife preparing food and managing the household while the wagoner performs wage labor outside the home. When his labor fails to generate sufficient income, her reproductive labor expands to compensate, illustrating how capitalist crises are absorbed within the household. The final image of the wagoner holding his crying child reverses the gendered division of labor established at the beginning of the film. He is no longer a worker transporting passengers through the city but a father confronted with the immediate consequences of economic dispossession. The crying infant underscores that labor is never solely about individual wages; it is about sustaining the conditions necessary for family survival. By ending on this image rather than the police encounter, Sembène locates the deepest effects of exploitation not in the workplace but within the intimate sphere of everyday life.

Labor here is not simply exploitation but a boundary-making process that determines which bodies are legible as economic actors and which are cast out of circulation altogether.

The spatial transition is not merely aesthetic; it exposes how colonial power survives through the unequal organization of the city, where movement, work, and belonging remain unevenly distributed after political independence.

This sequence reveals the full consequences of the wagoner's encounter with colonial authority. Having lost his cart to pay the fine, he has been separated from the means through which he earned his living. Although he still possesses the capacity to work, he no longer possesses the material instrument necessary to transform labor into income. This marks an important shift from individual grievance to structural critique. The wagoner recognizes that his suffering is not caused by isolated acts of dishonesty but by an economic and political system that repeatedly extracts labor without guaranteeing security. Karl Marx captures this contradiction when he writes: "The worker becomes all the poorer the more wealth he produces" (Marx 1844, 18). Throughout the film, the wagoner works continuously, transporting passengers, hauling construction materials, carrying the sick, and assisting the grieving, yet each act of labor leaves him no more secure than before. His final dispossession demonstrates that relentless work alone cannot overcome the structural inequalities that govern colonial and capitalist society.

The film ends where it began: within the household. This circular structure emphasizes that the failures of the day's labor are ultimately borne within the home. Although the wagoner has worked continuously, he returns unable to provide food or income, admitting, "I don't have anything for you. Not even a cart!" His inability to reproduce the conditions of his family's survival immediately shifts responsibility onto his wife, who leaves in search of food while he remains behind caring for the crying infant. This scene recalls the opening images of the wife performing domestic work. Throughout the film, her labor remains largely unwaged and invisible, yet it is essential to the family's survival. Sembène visualizes this insight by repeatedly showing the wife preparing food and managing the household while the wagoner performs wage labor outside the home. When his labor fails to generate sufficient income, her reproductive labor expands to compensate, illustrating how capitalist crises are absorbed within the household. The final image of the wagoner holding his crying child reverses the gendered division of labor established at the beginning of the film. He is no longer a worker transporting passengers through the city but a father confronted with the immediate consequences of economic dispossession. The crying infant underscores that labor is never solely about individual wages; it is about sustaining the conditions necessary for family survival. By ending on this image rather than the police encounter, Sembène locates the deepest effects of exploitation not in the workplace but within the intimate sphere of everyday life.
